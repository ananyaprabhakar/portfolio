Setting the Scene

A B2B ticketing startup. Real clients. Actual deadlines.

TicketsQue is a B2B digital ticketing startup building solutions for event management — digital coupons, ticket sales, and loyalty programs for pubs, venues, and event organizers. Small team, fast pace, real clients waiting on the other end.

I joined as a UI/UX Design Intern with a Computer Science degree and a Google UX certificate. No master's degree yet. No years of industry experience. Just eagerness and a Figma account.

Here's what they handed me: two separate design problems, a PM, a design lead, and a deadline.

Both shipped.

Chapter 1 — Echoozy Dashboard

The problem wasn't a missing dashboard. It was a dashboard built for no one in particular.

Echoozy is a co-working space company that needed a dashboard to help their internal team manage operations: track available workspaces, handle bookings, onboard new clients, monitor usage, and support sales — all in one place.

The problem wasn't that they had no dashboard. The problem was that the one they had wasn't built around the people who had to use it every day.

Two very different people, actually.

Meet the Users

What They Both Had in Common

Four pain points that kept surfacing — no matter which person you talked to.

01
Complexity over clarity
Too many features, not enough intuition. Every task took longer than it should.
02
No feedback loop
The dashboard didn't evolve with their needs. They worked around it instead of with it.
03
Integration friction
Getting the dashboard to talk to other tools was painful. Everything lived in silos.
04
Inconsistency
Different sections felt like they were designed by different people on different days.

Wireframes & IA

Paper first. Working out what Priya needs to see first, what Rahul needs to access quickly.

Before opening Figma, I sketched. The goal was to understand how two very different mental models could share an interface without either person feeling like it wasn't built for them.

Then Digital Wireframes

The wireframes focused on three things: a main landing dashboard with key stats visible at a glance, a persistent top search bar, and detail views for drilling into any category. Clean. Scannable. No hunting.

Main Dashboard

All key statistics on one screen. No clicking around to understand what's going on.

Detail Pages

All tracked category information, structured and scannable. No digging through sub-menus to find context.

Top Bar

Search + number of entries, always visible. The tools that both users reach for most go at the top.

Pagination

Because real data is never 5 rows. The interface needed to scale gracefully to hundreds of entries.

Profile Pages

Client profiles with all the details the team needs, plus a gallery view for workspace context.

The Decision

Same entry point. Completely different scope. So we built two.

Trying to force both roles into one view would have meant either overwhelming Priya with controls she didn't need, or restricting Rahul from data that was critical to his job. The IA made it clear: these aren't two people using the same tool differently. They're two people with fundamentally different access requirements.

For Priya — Workspace Manager
Admin Dashboard

Day-to-day operations. The view is built around what Priya needs to keep the space running without friction.

For Rahul — Sales & Partnerships
Super Admin Dashboard

Broader system access. The view is built around what Rahul needs to sell the space and manage enterprise relationships.

Usability Testing

Two rounds. Real users. Real feedback.

Designs don't get better in isolation. After the initial builds, we tested with real users, watched where they got stuck, and went back and fixed it.

Round 1 — What We Found

Navigation wasn't as intuitive as we thought

New admins were getting lost between sections. The structure made sense to us; it didn't make sense to them.

Feature overload was real

The Super Admin Dashboard had too much going on at once. Finding specific things took too long and felt overwhelming at first glance.

Staff onboarding needed more guidance

Adding new staff members during onboarding was confusing. First-time users needed clearer direction about what came next.

We went back and changed things.

Round 2 — What Changed

  • Navigation felt significantly more intuitive. Users moved through sections without hesitation.
  • The Super Admin Dashboard felt manageable with the reorganized feature layout. No more "where is that thing?"
  • New users moved through staff onboarding without getting stuck. The added tooltips and step guidance did their job.
50%
reduction in time to value for the client.

The Echoozy dashboard shipped to the client. Clear workspace tracking, two role-appropriate views, and a significantly smoother experience for the people who had to live in it every day.

Chapter 2 — VIP Membership Module

If Echoozy was about making a complex tool usable, this was about making a business case irresistible.

TicketsQue was expanding their product — a VIP membership program that pub owners could offer to their most loyal customers. Think exclusive perks, digital membership cards, loyalty rewards. Great idea. But pub owners needed to understand why it was worth adopting before they'd ever click "Book a Demo."

That's where I came in. I designed the page — and wrote the content for it.

The Problem

Pubs and venues were already managing a lot. Convincing them to add another tool to their stack meant the value proposition had to be immediate and undeniable. The page needed to answer three questions in under 30 seconds:

Question 1

What is this? The value proposition in plain language, before any scrolling.

Question 2

Why does my pub need it? Business benefits tied directly to what a pub owner actually cares about — retention, revenue, repeat visits.

Question 3

How does it actually work? A clear process flow, not a wall of feature specs.

What I Designed

Working with the design lead and PM through multiple rounds of iteration, the page came together around a clear hierarchy.

01
Hero
"Enhance Customer Loyalty and Drive Revenue with VIP Membership."

Big and direct. A visual that showed the product ecosystem at a glance, before any explanation.

02
Benefits Section
Six cards. Not a wall of text.

Customer Retention, Increased Revenue, Data Insights, Marketing Opportunities, Brand Loyalty, Community Building — each stated simply, each tied back to what a pub owner actually cares about.

03
How It Works
A step-by-step process flow. Visual, not verbal.

Distribute memberships → add offers and benefits → members enjoy benefits → business grows. Four steps. No jargon.

04
Features Sneak Peek
Concrete beats abstract every time.

A diagram showing what VIP cards could actually be used for: loyalty points, special discounts, complimentary items, early access. Show it, don't just list it.

05
FAQs
Four questions addressing the real hesitations.

What is this program, how does it work, what are the benefits, can businesses customise it. Objections answered before the sales call.

06
CTA
"Revolutionize Your Business with TicketsQue."

One clear next step. "Create Event." No ambiguity about what happens when you click.

The Iteration Reality

The design went through multiple rounds of review with the design lead and PM before it was signed off. Each session tightened the hierarchy, refined the content, and pushed the visual language toward something cleaner and more confident. Startup feedback loops are fast and direct — you present, you listen, you go back and fix it. That's exactly what this was.

25%
increase in sales after the VIP module launched.

A landing page that answered the right questions in the right order, written and designed by the same person, shipped after genuine iteration.

What I Learned
Three things that stuck.

I walked into TicketsQue thinking internships were about learning how to design. I walked out having learned something more specific.

How to design in conditions that are not ideal, with timelines that are not comfortable, for clients who are real and waiting. Three things that stuck:

Speed is a design constraint, not an excuse.

At a startup, "we don't have time for another round of research" is sometimes genuinely true. Learning to make good decisions quickly — with the information you have, not the information you wish you had — is a real skill. I got reps in on it here.

Feedback loops are everything.

The sessions with the design lead and PM were where the work got better. Not in my own head, not in Figma alone — in the conversation. I initiated those sessions. I'd do it again every time.

Shipping is different from finishing.

A design is never done. But at some point, it has to go live. The Echoozy dashboard and the VIP page both launched while I was still mentally tweaking them. That's fine. That's how it works. Done and shipped beats perfect and waiting.
